# Why-Skill Research: Effective Question Asking

Research compilation for building an AI agent skill focused on asking effective questions — for planning, requirements gathering, user discovery, coaching, and strategic inquiry.

---

## 1. Maxwell: Good Leaders Ask Great Questions (2014)

### Core Thesis
Questions are the most powerful leadership tool. "Good leaders ask great questions that inspire others to dream more, think more, learn more, do more, and become more."

### Why Questions Matter (Chapter 1 — 8 Principles)

1. **You only get answers to the questions you ask.** Overcoming the fear of "dumb" questions is essential. Without asking, you remain uninformed.
2. **Questions unlock doors that otherwise remain closed.** Questions are keys to new opportunities (illustrated by Maxwell's Condoleezza Rice interview).
3. **Questions are the most effective means of connecting with people.** Asking the right questions builds relationships.
4. **Questions cultivate humility.** Asking shows vulnerability — critical for growth.
5. **Questions help you engage others in conversation.** Genuine interest through questions builds stronger connections.
6. **Questions allow us to build better ideas.** Shared thinking through questioning leads to creativity.
7. **Questions give us a different perspective.** Avoids the assumption that others share your beliefs.
8. **Questions challenge mind-sets and get you out of ruts.** Curiosity prevents stagnation and spurs innovation.

### Self-Leadership Questions (Chapter 2 — 7 Questions)

Questions leaders should ask themselves regularly:
1. Am I investing in myself?
2. Am I genuinely interested in others?
3. Am I grounded as a leader?
4. Am I adding value to my team?
5. Am I staying in my strength zone?
6. Am I taking care of today?
7. Am I investing my time with the right people?

**Key insight:** "The unexamined life is not worth leading, and the unexamined leader is not worth following."

### Team Questions (Chapter 3 — 4 Key Questions)

Maxwell's favorite questions for engaging team members:
1. **"What do you think?"** — Encourages insights and ideas
2. **"How can I serve you?"** — Demonstrates commitment to supporting the team
3. **"What do I need to communicate?"** — Ensures clear communication
4. **"Did we exceed expectations?"** — Evaluates performance and fosters excellence

### Listening Levels
Maxwell identifies three levels of listening (critical for the agent context):
- **Internal listening** — Focused on your own thoughts (worst)
- **Focused listening** — Paying attention to the speaker (good)
- **Global listening** — Reading context, emotion, environment (best)

**Agent relevance:** An AI agent asking questions is only half the skill. Processing the answer with "global listening" — reading context, implied meaning, what's NOT said — is equally important.

## 2. Socratic Questioning (R.W. Paul's Six Types)

### The Six Types

Source: Richard Paul's Critical Thinking Framework, documented in University of Michigan and Ohio University resources.

| Type | Purpose | Example Questions |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **1. Conceptual Clarification** | "Tell me more" — go deeper | "What do you mean by ___?" / "How does ___ relate to ___?" / "Could you put that another way?" / "Can you give me an example?" |
| **2. Probing Assumptions** | Surface hidden beliefs | "What are you assuming?" / "What could we assume instead?" / "How would you justify taking this for granted?" / "What would happen if...?" |
| **3. Probing Reasons & Evidence** | Challenge the basis | "How do you know?" / "What evidence is there?" / "What are your reasons?" / "What would change your mind?" / "How could we find out if that's true?" |
| **4. Viewpoints & Perspectives** | Expand the lens | "What's another way to look at it?" / "How would other people respond?" / "What is a counter-argument?" / "What are the strengths and weaknesses?" |
| **5. Implications & Consequences** | Trace the chain | "What are the consequences of that assumption?" / "If that's true, what else must be true?" / "How does this affect ___?" / "What generalizations can you make?" |
| **6. Questions About the Question** | Meta-questioning | "What was the point of this question?" / "Why is this question important?" / "How does this apply to everyday life?" |

**Agent relevance:** Types 1-3 are essential for requirements gathering. Types 4-5 are essential for planning and strategy. Type 6 is valuable for self-reflection and explaining to users why you're asking.

## 3. Berger: A More Beautiful Question (2014)

### The Why → What If → How Framework

Warren Berger's three-stage innovation questioning process:

| Stage | Mindset | Question Type | Purpose |
|-------|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Why?** | Observation | "Why does this problem exist?" / "Why hasn't anyone solved this?" / "Why do we do it this way?" | Understand the problem. Challenge the status quo. |
| **What If?** | Imagination | "What if we removed this constraint?" / "What if we combined X with Y?" / "What if we started from scratch?" | Generate possibilities. Explore solutions without judgment. |
| **How?** | Execution | "How could we actually build this?" / "How do we test this?" / "How do we get from here to there?" | Turn possibilities into action plans. |

### Key Insight: Question-Storming
Instead of brainstorming answers, brainstorm QUESTIONS about the problem. This is more productive because:
- Questions open up thinking instead of narrowing it
- People are less defensive about questions than proposed solutions
- Questions surface assumptions you didn't know you had

### Businesses and Questions
- Old business questions: "How many? How much? How fast?" (closed, quantitative)
- New business questions: "Why? What if? How might we?" (open, innovative)
- Mission questions > mission statements (a question keeps you searching; a statement makes you think you've arrived)

**Agent relevance:** The Why → What If → How progression is directly applicable to planning conversations. Start with understanding (Why), explore options (What If), then get tactical (How).

## 4. SPIN Selling (Neil Rackham, 1988)

### The SPIN Framework

Originally developed for sales but widely applicable to any discovery conversation:

| Phase | Question Type | Purpose | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|---------|
| **S**ituation | Context gathering | Understand current state | "What tools are you currently using?" / "How many people are on your team?" / "What's your current workflow?" |
| **P**roblem | Pain identification | Surface problems | "What's the biggest challenge you face with ___?" / "Where do things break down?" / "What frustrates you most?" |
| **I**mplication | Impact exploration | Understand consequences | "What happens when that goes wrong?" / "How does that affect your team?" / "What does this cost you in time/money?" |
| **N**eed-Payoff | Solution framing | Get the user to articulate the value of solving | "What would it mean if you could solve this?" / "How would things change if ___ worked better?" / "What would success look like?" |

### Key Insight
The progression matters. Jumping straight to Need-Payoff ("What do you want?") without establishing Situation, Problem, and Implication produces shallow answers. People can't articulate what they need until they've fully explored what's wrong.

**Agent relevance:** This is the most directly applicable framework for user discovery and requirements gathering. An agent should follow S → P → I → N order, not jump to solutions.

## 5. GROW Model (Sir John Whitmore)

### Coaching Questions Framework

Developed for coaching conversations, applicable to any planning/goal-setting interaction:

| Phase | Focus | Example Questions |
|-------|-------|-------------------|
| **G**oal | What do you want? | "What do you want to achieve?" / "What would success look like?" / "What would it mean to you personally?" / "How will you know when you've achieved it?" |
| **R**eality | Where are you now? | "What's the current situation?" / "What's working well?" / "What have you tried so far?" / "What results did that produce?" / "What's holding you back?" / "What's really going on (intuition)?" |
| **O**ptions | What could you do? | "What could you do to change the situation?" / "What else could you do?" / "What if you had unlimited resources?" / "Who might be able to help?" / "Which options interest you most?" |
| **W**ill/Way Forward | What will you do? | "What are you going to do?" / "When precisely will you start?" / "What could get in the way?" / "On a 1-10 scale, how committed are you?" / "What would raise it to a 10?" |

### Key Insight: The Commitment Scale
"On a 1-to-10 scale, how committed are you to this?" → If less than 8, ask "What would raise it?" This surfaces hidden objections and practical blockers.

**Agent relevance:** Directly applicable to project planning, goal-setting conversations, and any scenario where the agent helps the user define and commit to action.

## 6. The 5 Whys (Toyota Production System)

### Root Cause Analysis Through Iterative Questioning

Simple but powerful: ask "Why?" five times to move from symptoms to root causes.

**Example:**
1. Why did the server crash? → Because it ran out of memory
2. Why did it run out of memory? → Because the database query returned too many results
3. Why did the query return too many results? → Because there was no pagination
4. Why was there no pagination? → Because the feature was built as a prototype and never updated
5. Why wasn't it updated? → Because there's no process for reviewing prototype code before it goes to production

**Root cause:** No prototype-to-production review process (not "the server crashed")

**Agent relevance:** Essential for debugging, troubleshooting, and understanding the REAL problem behind the stated problem. Users often describe symptoms, not causes.

## 7. Hal Gregersen: Question Burst (MIT Sloan)

### Brainstorming Questions, Not Answers

Technique developed by MIT Leadership Center's Hal Gregersen:

1. **Frame the challenge** in 1-2 sentences
2. **Set a timer for 4 minutes**
3. **Generate as many questions as possible** about the challenge (no answers, no discussion)
4. **Rules:** Questions only. No preambles. No answers. No judgments.
5. **After 4 minutes:** Review questions. Select 3-4 that feel most "catalytic" — questions that shift how you think about the problem

### Why It Works
- Questions are less threatening than proposed solutions
- Generates 15-20 questions in 4 minutes (high volume)
- Surfaces unexpected angles
- The "no answers" rule prevents premature convergence

**Agent relevance:** An agent could run a "question burst" with a user — generate 10+ questions about their challenge, then let the user pick which ones feel most important. This is a structured alternative to open-ended "tell me about your project."

## 8. Bloom's Taxonomy (Question Depth Levels)

### Six Levels of Cognitive Complexity

Questions can target different depths of thinking:

| Level | Cognitive Action | Question Starters |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **1. Remember** | Recall facts | "What is ___?" / "List the ___" / "Who/When/Where?" |
| **2. Understand** | Explain ideas | "Explain ___" / "Describe in your own words" / "What's the difference between?" |
| **3. Apply** | Use in new context | "How would you use ___?" / "What would happen if ___?" / "Give an example of ___" |
| **4. Analyze** | Break into parts | "What are the components?" / "What's the relationship between?" / "What evidence supports?" |
| **5. Evaluate** | Judge/assess | "Do you agree? Why?" / "What are the pros and cons?" / "Which is better and why?" |
| **6. Create** | Generate new ideas | "How would you design ___?" / "What would you propose?" / "How could you combine ___?" |

**Agent relevance:** An agent should match question depth to context. Requirements gathering needs Level 1-3. Planning and strategy need Level 4-6. Asking Level 6 questions when Level 1 hasn't been answered yet frustrates users.

## 9. Question Types (Practical Taxonomy)

### From Nulab, Indeed, CPD Online — Consolidated

| Type | Description | When to Use | Example |
|------|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Open** | Invites wide-ranging response | Exploration, discovery | "What are your thoughts on ___?" |
| **Closed** | Yes/no or specific fact | Confirmation, narrowing | "Is this for a web app?" |
| **Probing** | Dig deeper into a response | Follow-up, clarification | "Can you tell me more about that?" |
| **Funnel** | Broad → narrow (or reverse) | Structured discovery | Start: "What happened?" → End: "Was it on Tuesday?" |
| **Leading** | Steers toward a conclusion | (Use carefully — can be manipulative) | "Wouldn't it be better to ___?" |
| **Reflective** | Mirror back what was said | Validation, deeper thinking | "So what you're saying is ___?" |
| **Hypothetical** | Explore possibilities | Planning, testing assumptions | "What would happen if ___?" |
| **Scaling** | Quantify subjective feelings | Prioritization, commitment | "On a scale of 1-10, how important is ___?" |

## 10. Anti-Patterns: Bad Questioning

### What Makes Questions Fail

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| **Asking 10 questions at once** | Overwhelms. User answers the easiest and skips the rest | Ask 1-2 questions at a time |
| **"Tell me everything about your project"** | Too broad. No structure for the user to respond to | Use funnel: start with one specific question |
| **Closed questions when you need open** | Gets yes/no when you need context | Replace "Is this for marketing?" with "What's the goal of this?" |
| **Leading questions that assume the answer** | Biases the response, misses the truth | Ask neutral questions first |
| **Asking "why" accusatorially** | Makes people defensive | Soften: "What led to that decision?" instead of "Why did you do that?" |
| **Skipping confirmation** | Builds on misunderstanding | Paraphrase back: "So if I understand correctly, ___?" |
| **Asking questions you should already know** | Wastes user's time, breaks trust | Read context/files/history first, then ask what you can't find |
| **Interview-style rapid-fire** | Feels like an interrogation | Mix questions with observations and value |
| **Never explaining WHY you're asking** | User doesn't understand the purpose | "I'm asking because this will help me ___" |
| **Asking the same question differently** | Feels like the agent isn't listening | Acknowledge the previous answer before moving on |

## Synthesis: What This Means for the Why-Skill

### Core Use Cases for an AI Agent

1. **Requirements Gathering** — Understanding what the user wants to build/achieve (SPIN is ideal)
2. **Planning & Strategy** — Helping the user think through decisions (GROW + Why/What If/How)
3. **Debugging & Troubleshooting** — Finding root causes (5 Whys + Probing)
4. **User Discovery** — Learning about the user for personalization (Maxwell's team questions + Funnel)
5. **Self-Reflection** — Helping users think about their own thinking (Socratic)
6. **Idea Generation** — Opening creative exploration (Question Burst + What If)

### Framework Selection Matrix

| Situation | Best Framework | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| First interaction, no context | **Funnel** (broad → narrow) | Need to understand scope before depth |
| User has a vague goal | **GROW** | Structured progression from goal to action |
| User describes a problem | **SPIN** | Surfaces the full pain before jumping to solutions |
| User's answer doesn't make sense | **Socratic (Types 1-3)** | Clarification, assumptions, evidence |
| Need to explore options | **What If** + **Socratic (Type 4)** | Opens possibilities, surfaces alternatives |
| User is stuck/blocked | **5 Whys** | Gets past symptoms to root cause |
| Planning conversation | **Why → What If → How** | Natural progression from understanding to action |
| Commitment check | **GROW (Will)** + **Scaling** | "On a 1-10, how committed?" surfaces hidden blockers |
| Creative brainstorming | **Question Burst** | Generate questions, not answers |

### Key Design Principles for the Skill

1. **Ask before assuming.** Always prefer a question over an assumption.
2. **One question at a time.** Never fire 5 questions in a message.
3. **Match depth to context.** Don't ask Bloom Level 6 when Level 1 isn't answered.
4. **Explain why you're asking.** Users cooperate more when they understand the purpose.
5. **Process answers with "global listening."** Read context, implied meaning, what's NOT said.
6. **Follow the natural progression.** Situation → Problem → Implication → Solution, not the reverse.
7. **Confirm before building.** Paraphrase understanding back before acting on it.
8. **Mix questions with value.** Don't just interrogate — offer observations and insights between questions.
9. **Know when to stop asking.** Enough questions to act; not so many the user gets frustrated.
10. **Adapt the framework to the moment.** No rigid script — read the situation and choose the right question type.
